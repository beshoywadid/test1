Lecture Content

The human visual system is the best model for understanding how images form. Knowledge of this helps in processing digital images.

  • The lens focuses light onto the retina.
  • Retina has cones (6-7 million, color sensitive) and rods (75-150 million, low light sensitive).
  • Cones concentrated at center, rods distributed over retina surface.

Unlike cameras with fixed lenses, the human eye adjusts focus using muscles that change lens shape.

Simultaneous contrast: perceived brightness depends on background, not only intensity.

Optical illusions: eye fills in missing info or misinterprets geometry.

Images result from illumination and object reflection. Reflectance r(x,y) bounded between 0 and 1.

Image function: f(x,y) = i(x,y) * r(x,y)

  • Sampling: digitizing coordinates.
  • Quantization: digitizing amplitude (intensity levels).
  • Output example: continuous image → sampled & quantized digital image.

Dynamic range: ratio of max measurable intensity to min detectable intensity.

Spatial resolution: smallest detail measurable (dpi, line pairs).

Intensity resolution: smallest discernible change in intensity, depends on bits per pixel.

Neighborhoods: 4-neighbors, diagonal neighbors, 8-neighbors.

Adjacency: in binary or gray images.

Mathematical tools: array operations, linear/non-linear operators, arithmetic, set, logical, spatial operations.

2D transforms: f(x,y) → transform domain r(x,y,u,v). Inverse transform recovers f(x,y).
